# Evidence and research

## Source priority

1. User-provided primary materials and required references.
2. Original papers, official datasets, standards, laws, institutional reports, and product documentation.
3. High-quality reviews or textbooks for synthesis and framing.
4. Reputable secondary reporting only when primary evidence is unavailable or context requires it.

Do not invent citations, DOI values, datasets, internal claims, or inaccessible evidence. Mark inference as inference.

## Academic presentation principles

Use these evidence-backed practices as design rationale, not visible slide content unless requested:

- Plan the argument before designing pages; a talk is linear and should not mirror a paper mechanically.
- Make each slide convey one point, ideally stated by its title.
- Introduce charts/figures so the audience knows axes, encodings, and the pattern to notice.
- Use sufficient font size and contrast for the actual viewing environment.
- Maintain consistent format while varying layouts according to content.

## Slide citations

- Cite sources close to the claim, figure, or table they support.
- Prefer short author–year or numbered citations on slides and a full references section or notes when needed.
- For multi-paper reviews, keep a consistent paper identifier across all slides.
- For reproduced figures, include source and adaptation status.
- If a source cannot be verified, omit the claim or flag it for the user rather than presenting it as fact.

## Figures and data

- Rebuild charts from source data when possible; preserve exact values and units.
- Do not redraw scientific figures in ways that change meaning.
- Use direct annotation to explain the relevant signal.
- Separate observed result, interpretation, and recommendation.
- Keep a private source ledger during production so revisions do not detach claims from evidence.
