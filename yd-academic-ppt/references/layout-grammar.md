# Layout grammar

Choose composition by semantic relationship. Vary central layouts while preserving the outer design system.

## Core archetypes

### A. Minimal cover

Title, subtitle, author/institution/date, and one restrained thesis line. Do not use a dense card grid.

### B. Roadmap / section architecture

Use 4–6 numbered modules connected by a clear reading path. State what each section accomplishes, not just its name.

### C. Dominant thesis + evidence

Large claim on one side; chart, figure, formula, or 2–3 evidence blocks on the other. Use for definitions, central findings, and implications.

### D. Two-sided comparison

Use identical row logic and aligned baselines. Add a bottom-line judgment. Suitable for methods, theories, alternatives, or before/after results.

### E. Three-part explanation

Three equal or weighted columns: what, why, how; problem, mechanism, consequence; or input, model, output.

### F. Four-stage decision grid

Four numbered steps or categories in a 2×2 layout. Connect only when sequence matters.

### G. Five-to-seven category strip

Use narrow repeated columns only for short, parallel items such as risks or criteria. Limit each column to one icon, one short header, and 2–3 brief lines.

### H. Process / pipeline

Use numbered stages, arrows, and optional feedback loops. Keep labels verb-led. Put decisions in diamonds only when branching is real.

### I. Chart-led result

Give 55–70% of the slide to the chart. Add one highlighted inference and 1–3 interpretation bullets. Never shrink a chart to decorate a text page.

### J. Table / matrix

Use for exact comparison across at least three repeated dimensions. Limit columns to what can be read at presentation distance. Place the table inside a rounded editorial card with a navy title/header band and compact zebra rows. When the table shares a page with a chart or interpretation, target roughly 35–45% of the content width instead of stretching it edge-to-edge. Highlight the conclusion, not every cell.

### K. Formula / model explanation

Show the equation or architecture, then define symbols, intuition, assumptions, and use. Avoid displaying an equation without interpretation.

### L. Worked example / case

Setup → operation/evidence → result → what it means. Use consistent numeric precision and show the key reasoning step.

### M. Literature / paper card

For each paper capture: research problem, data/context, model/method, key result, contribution, limitation, and relevance to the deck thesis. Avoid bibliographic summaries with no analytical link.

### N. Synthesis map

Show shared premise, complementary roles, disagreements, causal/mechanistic links, and the remaining gap. Use a table when relationships are exact; use a diagram only when topology matters.

### O. Conclusion / action

Use 3–5 claims or actions. Each should answer “so what?” Include limitations or next steps when academically appropriate.

## Density budgets

- One slide = one primary takeaway.
- Aim for 30–90 visible Chinese characters on standard presentation slides; allow more for deliberate handout-style teaching pages only when body text remains readable.
- Use no more than 3–5 bullets in a region and no more than two text hierarchy levels inside a card.
- Keep a minimum of 8–12% of the canvas visually quiet.
- A card grid is justified only when items are parallel and independently scannable.
- If a table needs tiny type, split it, simplify dimensions, or move the full table to an appendix.
- In template-fidelity mode, do not confuse whitespace with quality: the reference is information-rich. Use the available central canvas deliberately with 2–4 aligned regions, supporting icons/labels, and a takeaway strip when appropriate. Large unused zones on ordinary content slides are a defect.

## Rhythm rules

- Alternate dense and open slides.
- Do not use the same archetype on more than two consecutive slides unless comparing a series.
- Place a chart/figure-led slide at least every 3–5 slides when evidence supports it.
- Use section dividers sparingly; a strong title and eyebrow often provide enough orientation.
- Close each section with a synthesis, decision rule, or implication rather than a decorative transition.
